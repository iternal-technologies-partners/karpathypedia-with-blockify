Inconsistent quality is a recognized challenge in manual document review processes, arising from the inherent variability introduced when human analysts are responsible for accuracy-dependent tasks. The core problem stems from the fact that human performance is not uniform, and several interrelated factors cause the standard of work to fluctuate across individuals, time periods, and working conditions.

## Overview

When document review relies on human effort, the quality of outcomes is directly tied to the capabilities and state of the individuals performing the work. Unlike automated systems, human reviewers bring personal variation to every task they undertake. This means that two analysts reviewing identical documents may produce different results, and even a single analyst may produce different results when reviewing similar documents at different points in time.

## Contributing Factors

### Analyst Variability

One of the primary drivers of inconsistent quality is the difference in ability, experience, and judgment between individual analysts. Human-dependent accuracy varies by analyst, meaning that the same document reviewed by different people may be assessed differently. This inter-analyst variability makes it difficult to guarantee a uniform standard across a large review process, particularly when teams are composed of individuals with differing levels of expertise or familiarity with the subject matter.

### Fatigue

Fatigue is a significant contributing factor to declining quality in manual document review. As analysts work through large volumes of material, their concentration and accuracy can diminish over time. Human-dependent accuracy is known to vary by fatigue level, which means that documents reviewed later in a session or workday may receive less rigorous attention than those reviewed at the outset. This creates an uneven distribution of review quality that is difficult to detect or correct after the fact.

### Time Pressure

The conditions under which review work is performed also play a meaningful role in quality outcomes. When analysts operate under time pressure, their capacity to apply careful, thorough scrutiny to each document is reduced. Human-dependent accuracy varies with time pressure, suggesting that deadlines and workload demands can lead analysts to make decisions more quickly and with less deliberation, increasing the likelihood of errors or omissions.

## Implications for Document Review Processes

The combined effect of analyst variability, fatigue, and time pressure means that manual document review is inherently susceptible to producing uneven results. A document reviewed under ideal conditions — by a rested, experienced analyst working at a comfortable pace — may receive markedly different treatment than one reviewed under suboptimal circumstances. This unpredictability poses challenges for organizations that require consistent, reliable outcomes from their review processes.

Because inconsistency is tied to human factors that are difficult to eliminate entirely, it represents a structural limitation of manual review methodologies. Organizations must account for the reality that quality will not be uniform across large review projects and that results may be influenced by factors entirely unrelated to the content of the documents themselves.

## Relationship to Human-Dependent Accuracy

The concept of inconsistent quality is fundamentally linked to the broader idea of human-dependent accuracy — the principle that the correctness and reliability of a manual process is a function of the humans performing it. This dependency creates a ceiling on consistency, since human performance is subject to natural variation in ways that mechanical or automated processes are not. Recognizing this relationship is important for accurately evaluating the reliability of any review process that depends on human judgment.
